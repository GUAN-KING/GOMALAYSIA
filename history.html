A Brief History of Malaysia: Key Periods and Landmark Buildings



A seated Bodhisattva carved in terracotta, from site 21/22

1. Ancient Malay Kingdoms (circa 2nd century BCE - 15th century CE)

•  Malaysia has been inhabited since at least the 2nd century BCE, with early Malay kingdoms forming in areas such as Kedah and Penang.

Representative Building: Bujang Valley

•  The Bujang Valley (Malay: Lembah Bujang) is a sprawling historical complex.

•  The area consists of ruins that may date more than 1,500 years old. More than 50 ancient pagoda temples, called candi (pronounced as "chandi")

View on Google Maps (The Bujang Valley)


Malacca Sultanate Palace Museum

2. Malacca Sultanate (1400-1511)

•  In 1400, Parameswara established the Malacca Sultanate, becoming a major trading and Islamic center.

Representative Building: Malacca Sultanate Palace Museum

•  The Malacca Sultanate was the first significant sultanate in Malaysian history.
The Malacca Sultanate Palace Museum symbolizes its power and cultural heritage.

View on Google Maps (Malacca Sultanate Palace Museum)




A Famosa

3. Portuguese Occupation (1511-1641)

•  In 1511, the Portuguese captured Malacca, controlling key trade routes.

Representative Building: St. Paul's Hill and A Famosa

•  During the Portuguese occupation of Malacca, numerous churches and fortifications were built. St. Paul's Hill and A Famosa are among the most iconic buildings from this period.

View on Google Maps (St. Paul's Hill)

View on Google Maps (A Famosa)


Stadthuys

4. Dutch Rule (1641-1824)

•  In 1641, the Dutch defeated the Portuguese and became the new rulers of Malacca.

Representative Building: Stadthuys

•  During the Portuguese occupation of Malacca, numerous churches and fortifications were built. St. Paul's Hill and A Famosa are among the most iconic buildings from this period.

View on Google Maps (Stadthuys)


Kuala Lumpur Railway Station

5. British Colonial Era (1824-1957)

•  After the Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1824, much of Malaysia came under British colonial rule, leading to the development of infrastructure and modern education systems.

Representative Building: Kuala Lumpur Railway Station

•  Under British colonial rule, Malaysia's infrastructure developed significantly. The Kuala Lumpur Railway Station is a notable example of British colonial architecture.

View on Google Maps (Kuala Lumpur Railway Station)


National Mosque (Masjid Negara)

6. Independence and Modernization (1957-present)

•  Malaysia gained independence from Britain on August 31, 1957.
On September 16, 1963, Malaysia was officially formed, experiencing rapid economic growth and modernization.


Representative Building: National Mosque (Masjid Negara) and Petronas Towers

•  After Malaysia gained independence in 1957, the National Mosque became a symbol of independence and national unity.
The Petronas Towers, built in the late 1990s, represent modernization and economic development.
